ONCE UPON A TIME – long, long ago – there was a queen who in winter was sitting by the open window embroidering. 'Ouch!' she cried suddenly, for

she had pricked her finger. A drop of blood fell on the snow under the window. 'I wish, I wish,' said the queen, 'that I had a daughter with a skin as white as this snow, with hair as black as the ebony of this window-frame, and with lips as red as this drop of blood.' And what do you think? After a while a daughter was

born, and the girl did indeed have hair as black as ebony, a skin as white as snow, and lips as red as blood. So the king and queen were quite happy, you understand, and they called their daughter Snow White.

 

 

But one day the queen became ill, even so badly that she died. The king, who did not like it, so alone, after a while married again.

His new wife was very beautiful, but what the king did not know was that she thought of nothing else all day long but being beautiful. In her room she even had a magic mirror for that purpose.

 

Every morning she would stand in front of this mirror and ask:

 

Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?

 

And because it was a magic mirror, he could talk, and he always answered:

 

You are the most beautiful in the land .

 

 

The queen then sat down contentedly on a chair in front of her dressing table to  make herself even more beautiful.

But one day things went differently. The queen stood in front of the mirror again and asked:

 

 

Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?

And do you know what she heard now?

 

Oh, Queen, you are the most beautiful in this room,

but Snow White is a thousand times more beautiful than you.

 

The queen could not believe her ears. She pushed aside the curtain and saw Snow White playing, and knew at once that the mirror was right. 'No one must be more beautiful than I,' the queen growled. And then she thought of something very evil.

 

She called the palace hunter to her and gave him a large knife. 'Tomorrow you will take Snow White into the forest,' she said, 'and there you will stab her to death with this knife.' Then she gave him a small box.

"And to make sure she's dead, I want this box back with Snow White's heart in it."

 

 

The next day the huntsman went with Snow White into the forest. But when he took out his knife, he felt sorry for her. 'Go away, Snow White,' he said, 'as far away as possible, because the queen wants me to kill you.' Frightened, Snow White ran as fast as she could, deep into the forest. And do you know what the huntsman did? He killed a wild boar and put its heart in the queen's box.

 

 

Meanwhile, Snow White kept running. She ran over seven mountains until, exhausted, she came to a tiny house. The front door was not locked.

Snow White had to duck when she went inside.

There must be seven children living here, she thought. Look, there are seven little chairs, seven little plates, seven little glasses and seven little spoons. She

walked up a small narrow staircase and at the top she saw seven little beds in a row. Suddenly she felt how tired she was. (Yes, what do you expect, when you have run so hard over seven mountains!) And before she knew it she was stretched out across three beds and fell into a deep sleep.

 

Because of that she didn't hear the residents coming home. They weren't seven

children but seven dwarfs, and they saw right away that someone had been in the house. They crept upstairs and discovered the sleeping Snow White.


"Oh, how sweet she is," said one dwarf. "And how beautiful," said another.

"Shhh, don't wake them!" said a third. And they quietly crept back down the stairs.

 

 

 

After a long time Snow White woke up and when she found the seven dwarfs downstairs, she told them what had happened to her. The dwarfs had heard of the evil queen. 'They say she can do magic,' they whispered and crept closer together.

 

The next morning the dwarves had to go to the silver mine to work as usual. At the door they said to Snow White: 'Be careful, don't let anyone in. You never know!' They waved to her and disappeared behind the mountains.

 

 

 

Meanwhile, in the palace, the hunter had given the box to the evil queen.

 

"Now I'm the most beautiful again," she said with satisfaction and quickly went to stand in front of the magic mirror.

 

Mirror, mirror on the wall, who is the fairest of them all?

 

The mirror replied:

 

Oh, Queen, you are the most beautiful in this room,

but Snow White is a thousand times more beautiful than you.

 

The queen showed the box to the mirror. 'Ha! That can't be!

Snow White is dead. Here is her heart!' But the mirror answered:

 

Oh, Queen, the fairest in this room are you, but Snow White beyond the Seven Mountains in the house of the

Seven Dwarfs is a thousand times fairer than you!

 

The queen understood that the huntsman had fooled her. She trembled with rage.


“Then I'll kill her myself,” she said, gritting her teeth.

 

She went down six flights of stairs to the cellar and there conjured up a poisoned apple. It was red on one side and green on the other. She changed herself into an old woman and set off, over the seven mountains, to the house of the seven dwarves.

"I'll get her!" she growled again and again. And in her basket, among the other apples, lay the poisoned apple... Snow White, who was left alone in

the cottage of the seven dwarfs, was just about to put a pan of soup on the fire, when there was a tap at the window. It was an old woman, who was carrying a basket of apples.

 

'May I rest here for a while? I am so tired,' she panted. Snow White hesitated. She opened the window and said, 'I am sorry, old woman, the dwarves will not let anyone in. But I can give you a glass of water.' When the witch had

finished the glass, she said, 'Since you have been so kind to me, I will give you an apple. Look, a very beautiful red-green apple. Take a nice

bite!' Snow White took a bite and… fell dead on the ground.

 

 

 

'Hahahaha,' laughed the wicked queen (for that is what it was of course!), 'hahahaha! Now I am the fairest of them all again!' And she hurried back to her

palace, on the other side of the seven mountains.

When the dwarfs came home they found Snow White lying dead on the floor. The dwarfs were deeply and deeply sad, so sad that they could not say goodbye to her. They made a beautiful glass coffin for her and put her in it, so that they could always look at her.

 

A few days later a prince happened to be riding through the forest on his horse and saw the glass coffin. He thought Snow White was so beautiful that he lifted the glass lid and gave her a kiss.

 

Apparently Snow White was not completely dead, because she started coughing in fright and the poisoned piece of apple shot out of her throat.

She sat up in surprise and asked, "Where am I? What happened?"


“Hurray!” shouted the dwarves.

How happy they were that Snow White was alive again! The prince was too. He took Snow White on his horse and they rode off together to his palace.

There they got married and lived happily ever after.

 

And the evil queen? She heard from the mirror that Snow White was still the most beautiful. She found that so terrible that she dropped dead.

Yes, really, she fell over in sheer rage, just like that, right there in front of the mirror.

In a lonely dark place, the witch left Rapunzel alone in the woods, without food and without drink.

---

The witch quickly returned to the tower. She climbed up the braids, brought them inside, and stood fuming in the middle of the room. With her hands on her hips, she hissed: “So, prince, come on. Then you'll see what happens!”

---

When evening came, she heard him calling:

Rapunzel, Rapunzel, dear child,
Throw down your hair in a while!

---

The witch threw down the braids, and the prince climbed up unsuspectingly. When he wanted to step inside, the witch gave him a hard blow on the head and pushed him out the window. He fell into a bramble bush, landed with his eyes in the thorns, and then he was blind. The story keeps getting sadder...

---

The prince wandered around in the great forest, and somewhere else in that same forest, Rapunzel wandered as well. They ate berries and drank from forest brooks. This went on for years.

---

But then things happily turned brighter again, because one day, they happened to come across each other. He heard a girl singing and immediately recognized Rapunzel's voice. Rapunzel cried terribly when she saw that the prince was blind. Her tears fell onto his eyes and... suddenly he could see again.

Together they went to his castle, where they married and lived happily ever after.

They certainly deserved that after so much misery!

 

**Once upon a time**, there was a soldier who returned from a war. At home, he was told that his father had died. His brothers had divided everything their father owned among themselves. So there was nothing left for the soldier. The oldest brother felt sorry for him and gave him a silver coin as a consolation. One silver coin was all he received. The soldier put the silver coin in his pocket. Since he no longer had a home, he set off into the wide world.

---

One evening, he came to a large forest. An old, thin man in torn clothes asked him if he had any money, as he was very hungry. The soldier thought: no way! I only have that silver coin, and I want to buy bread with it. But he saw how the old man was trembling from hunger. So he took out the coin and said, "Here, please, I am young and strong; I can go a day without food."

At that moment, the old man turned into a young prince. Instead of rags, he was now wearing a beautiful outfit, sparkling with pearls and gemstones.

---

"I am the wizard of this forest," he said, "and because you wanted to give me your coin, I will reward you for your kindness. You may make a wish, and I will fulfill it. Just tell me what you want!"

The young man thought for a moment and then said, "I have always wanted to know what it is like to be an animal. I wish to be able to turn into a hare, a bird, or a fish." "That is a beautiful wish," said the wizard, "and your wish is already granted." And at that moment, he vanished into thin air.

---

Can I really turn into a hare? the young man thought. And yes, indeed: there he was, a hare. And just like that, he was a fish for a moment, and a little later, a bird!

--

Satisfied, the young man continued on his way and soon arrived in a village. There, the king was visiting, looking for soldiers to wage war against another country. "Would you like to become a soldier in my army?" he asked the young man. The young man was eager to do so, as he had been a soldier before, and at least he would earn some money.

---

Then came the day when the king summoned all the soldiers. It was time to start the war. The next morning, they departed at dawn.

Now, you should know that this king had nothing to fear. He possessed a very special ring that ensured he won all his wars. So, when they saw the enemy in the distance after three days, the king was not worried at all.

---

The enemy stood in ten tight rows, ready for battle. Just as the king was about to give the signal to attack, he discovered that he did not have his ring with him. It was at home on his nightstand!

Retrieving the ring would take three days there and three days back; that would take six days. Four, perhaps, if someone was very fast. That was far too long. What if the enemy attacked? The king was at a loss and desperately cried out, "Whoever can fetch the ring for me and return before evening may marry my daughter!"

---

Our soldier stepped forward and said he would give it a try. He left the camp, hid behind a rock, quickly transformed into a hare, and jumped away. But someone had seen what had happened: an older soldier who was jealous of him because the young man belonged to the royal guard and he did not.

The young man ran like a hare over hills and through valleys. When he reached a river, he transformed into a fish and shot through the water like an arrow from a bow. At a mountain, he turned into a bird, and shortly after, he flew into the princess's room. There, he transformed back into himself.

---

The princess was a little startled when she suddenly saw a strange young man in her room, but he quickly explained why he had come, and she understood there was no time to lose. She hurried to her father's nightstand.

When she handed him the ring, they looked deeply into each other’s eyes and immediately fell deeply in love.

Then the young man did something very clever that would help him later on. "Pay close attention," he said, "and do exactly as I ask." And he transformed into a hare. "Cut a piece from my tail," he said.

---

Then he quickly transformed into a fish. "Pull two scales from my back," he said. And finally, he changed into a bird. "Pluck two feathers from my wing," he said, "and keep all these things safe." And away he flew out the window, back to the king.

---

On the way, he transformed back into a fish and finally arrived as a hare at the camp, with the ring clamped between his teeth. The jealous soldier saw the hare coming, grabbed his rifle, and... shot!

The creature tumbled over. Dead as a doornail. The jealous soldier quickly snatched the ring from its mouth and handed it to the king. He put the ring on his finger, and you guessed it: he won the war.

Back in the palace, the king went with the jealous soldier to his daughter. "Thanks to this good soldier, we have won the war," he said to her. "So he may marry you. I promised him that. The wedding is the day after tomorrow."

The princess began to cry. She said that the man was a liar, but the king did not believe her. The princess continued to cry, even on the day of the wedding, and well... a crying bride, that simply could not be, so the celebration was postponed by a day.

---

Meanwhile, the wizard of the forest looked into his crystal ball and saw the hare dead in the field. He rubbed the ball, made a few magical gestures, and said, "Become alive again, hare! Get up and hurry to your princess, for she is marrying someone else tomorrow."

The hare blinked his eyes, jumped up, and ran to the palace. On the way, he first transformed back into a fish and then into a bird until he ultimately stood before the king as a soldier. "I have come to claim my reward," he said. "I fetched the ring for you, so I may marry your daughter. You said so yourself."

"That is not true," said the king. "I received the ring from another soldier, not from you." But he was not entirely sure of his case, so he called for his daughter. She immediately threw her arms around the soldier.

---

"Father," she cried joyfully, "this is the soldier who fetched the ring! Not the other one. The other one is a fraud."

"Who should I believe now?" said the king, completely confused. The princess ran to her room and came back with a golden box. She took out the tuft of hare's tail. The soldier transformed into a hare. Now the king could see that the tuft of tail fit perfectly into the bald spot on the hare's tail.

---

Then the princess produced the two scales. The soldier transformed into a fish. The king could see for himself that two scales were missing from the back.

Finally, the king saw with his own eyes that the bird was missing two feathers in its wings. And the princess held those two feathers in her hand...

The fraud received a hundred lashes and was thrown out of the palace, never to show himself again.

The soldier married his princess. A few years later, the king died, and the soldier became king.

---

Sometimes, when he wanted to see how things were going in his kingdom, he flew like a dove over his land, or looked like a fish from the water, or hid like a hare among the bushes. In this way, he learned all sorts of things and could take good care of his people.

He became the best king the land had ever had.

 

Once upon a time — long, long ago — there was a fisherman who was so poor that he lived with his wife in an upside-down flower pot. The fisherman didn’t mind this; it was just how it was, and in fact, it was quite cozy. Every morning, he walked to the sea at dawn, and he only returned home late in the afternoon with the fish he had caught. He never experienced anything special until one certain day...

---

On that day, he caught a fish that could talk. "If you throw me back into the sea, I will reward you," said the fish. "You may make a wish, and I will fulfill it."

The man was, as mentioned, a content man, so he said, “Oh little flounder” — because that’s what he had caught: a flounder, which is a type of fish — “little flounder, what should I wish for? I have a wife, I have food to eat every day, and I have a roof over my head. I don’t need anything more.” After saying this, he threw the flounder back into the sea. The fish disappeared underwater, and the fisherman went home. That evening, when he and his wife were in bed, he told her about the fish.

“Could you really make a wish?” asked his wife. “And what did you wish for?”
“Nothing,” said the man. “We have nothing to wish for.”

---

That night, the wife thought long and hard, and the next morning she said, “Actually, I do have something to wish for.”
“Oh,” said the man, surprised, “what is it?”
“I would like to live in a real house like everyone else,” said the wife, “instead of in an upside-down flower pot.”
“Do you mean that I should ask the flounder for a house?” asked the man.
“Yes,” said the wife, “a small, simple house with walls, windows, a roof, and a chimney. It doesn’t have to be big.”

---

The fisherman loved to see his wife happy, so the next day he went to the sea, gathered his courage, and called out:

“Flounder, flounder in the sea,
Here is little man Timpetee.
My dearest wife Elsebil
Wants something that I do not.”

---

The sea was calm, there was not a breath of wind, and nothing happened at all…


---

The man was about to go home when the flounder poked its head above the water and asked, “Well, what does she want?”
“Uh,” said the man hesitantly, “she would like to live in a real house…”
“Go home,” said the flounder. “She’s already living in a house.” And it was gone again, underwater.

“I’ll be damned,” said the man, and he went straight home. And yes, there stood, instead of the upside-down flower pot, a real little house with straight walls, a roof, a chimney, and windows with cheerful curtains. Everything was neatly painted. His wife came to meet him with open arms.
“Isn’t it a wonderful little house?” she exclaimed joyfully. “And just look how cozy it is inside. This is certainly something else compared to an upside-down flower pot.”
The man had to admit that. It had been dark as night in the flower pot all day, and now light streamed in through the three windows. There was space for a table and two chairs, and — what a surprise! — there was even an extra little room for sleeping.

The wife was over the moon with happiness. Singing, she kept the little house tidy. But after a few weeks, she said one evening in bed to her husband, “This house is quite cozy and nice, but it is also very cramped. We could use a bit more space. If you could ask the flounder for a bigger house tomorrow…”
“But I was only allowed to make one wish,” said the man.
“Oh, one wish more or less,” said the wife, “what does it matter? After all, he owes his life to you.” His wife might have had a point, so when he stood by the sea the next morning, he called out:

“Flounder, flounder in the sea,
Here is little man Timpetee.
My dearest wife Elsebil
Wants something that I do not.”

---

The man waited anxiously. The sea was choppy, and yes, thankfully, there was the flounder.
“Well, what does she want now?” it asked.
“Uh,” said the man, “she is very happy with the little house, but it is quite cramped. She would actually really like a bit more space…”
“That’s fine,” said the flounder. “Go home, she is already living in a big house.” And he disappeared again into the waves.

---

And what a big house it was! It had not three but ten large tall windows and a wide terrace. His wife was waiting for him, beaming: “Well, what do you think? Quite different from that tiny hut, wouldn’t you say?”
And that was true. The living room was ten times bigger. “See!” said the wife. “Aren’t you glad you asked for it?”
“Yes, that was a good idea,” said the man, “but are you satisfied now?”
“Yes, what do you think!” said the wife, proudly showing him five large bedrooms.

---

But after two weeks, the wife said, “This house is beautiful and large, but it all needs to be kept clean, and that’s no fun.”
“What do you want then?” asked the man, startled.
“A few maids, for example, to help me,” said the wife, “and also ask the flounder for a cook and someone to do the laundry, and someone for the garden too.”

---

The next day there was a strong wind, and the waves crashed high against the rocks. The fisherman stood by the sea and called out in the wind:

“Flounder, flounder in the sea,
Here is little man Timpetee.
My dearest wife Elsebil
Wants something that I do not.”

---

“Well, what does she want now?” asked the flounder, who immediately came up.
“Well,” said the man, “the house is really very spacious, and we are very happy with that, but it also means a lot of work. She thought that if she had some help…”
“Go home,” said the flounder, “she already has help.”
His wife was standing at the front door. She clapped her hands twice, and twelve servants came out and stood neatly in a row.
“Look,” said the wife, “they do all the work now.”
“Well, well,” said the man, “did you ever think you would have twelve servants one day? Now you must be satisfied.”
“Who wouldn’t be?” said the wife. She folded her hands over her chest and looked content as the servants went to work.

---

A few days went by quietly, but then the wife started complaining again. “I like being in charge of people, but it’s a pity there are only twelve of them. I would like to be in charge of many more people.” And after thinking for another day, she said, “Man, I want to be queen! Then I will be in charge of a lot of people. And I want a palace. Ask the flounder for that tomorrow.”

The man protested vehemently, but the wife said angrily that she would be very unhappy if she didn’t become queen. So, what do you do?

---

The next morning, it was very windy. The trees swayed back and forth, and the waves had white caps. The man had to shout to make himself heard:

“Flounder, flounder in the sea,
Here is little man Timpetee.
My dearest wife Elsebil
Wants something that I do not.”

---

The flounder appeared again. “Well,” it said, “what does she want now?”
“She wants to become queen,” said the man with his head bowed, “and she wants a palace…”
“Go home,” said the flounder. “She is already queen, and she has a palace.”

And indeed… There stood a large snow-white palace shining in the sun, and inside sat his wife on a throne, in a long dress of red velvet. Around her shoulders was a long red cloak. On her head, she wore a golden crown. Along the walls stood footmen and ladies-in-waiting, all waiting for what the queen would command. The fisherman approached his wife hesitantly.
“Wife,” he said in awe, “you look wonderful! Are you really a queen now? Does everyone have to do what you say? Well, now you must be truly satisfied.”
His wife said nothing; she merely nodded to a footman. He showed him around the palace. It had so many halls and rooms that the man lost count.

---

After a few days, when they were lying in the great royal bed in the evening, the fisherman's wife — who was now queen — sat up straight. “If the flounder can make me queen,” she said, “he can also make me empress, and then I’ll be in charge of everyone! Man, tomorrow you tell your flounder that I want to be empress.”
The man wanted to say something, but the wife shook him and shouted, “I am queen, and you are just a poor fisherman. So you just do what I say, understood?”

--

With lead in his shoes, the man walked to the sea the next day. The waves were towering, and the wind was roaring. The man shouted:

“Flounder, flounder in the sea,
Here is little man Timpetee.
My dearest wife Elsebil
Wants something that I do not.”

---

After a very long time, the flounder came up. “Well, what does she want this time?”
“She… she wants to become empress,” said the fisherman softly. He just hoped the flounder didn’t hear him.
“Go home,” said the flounder. “She is already empress.” And it disappeared into the raging waves.

---

Now there stood a palace as large as a village! The man had to ask for directions to the throne room, which was now much bigger. All the way at the other end, his wife sat on a high golden throne, her cloak shimmering and sparkling with gemstones. The hall was filled with kings, counts, and dukes, all bowing before her. His wife was now the most powerful in the whole land.

---

That evening, from the imperial bed, the woman looked out the window and saw the moon rise. “Everyone does what I want,” she said to herself, “even the king does what I want. But that moon, it does what it wants.” She shook the fisherman awake. “Man, I want the moon to do what I want. Get up, right now! Go to your flounder and tell him I want to be God.”

---

“Be God?” stammered the man. “That’s impossible…” He couldn’t get any further because his wife had already kicked him out of bed.

---

Black clouds had covered the moon, a storm was rising, rain lashed against his face, the sea churned as if stirred with a giant spoon. The wind almost blew him over. The fisherman whispered:

“Flounder, flounder in the sea,
Here is little man Timpetee.
My dearest wife Elsebil
Wants something that I do not.”

---

“Well?” said the flounder. “What does she want now?”
“She… she… wants to be God…” whispered the fisherman.
“Go home,” said the flounder. “She’s back in a flower pot.”
